Yes! Neurima fully supports high-resolution audio files (ALAC, FLAC) at sample rates up to 384kHz. For USB DACs, Neurima automatically negotiates the highest sample rate your DAC supports. Once a high rate is achieved (like 96kHz), it stays there to avoid iOS audio quirks with rate switching. For Bluetooth, iOS limits all Bluetooth audio to 44.1–48kHz using AAC codec, regardless of headphone capabilities. For Mac 3.5mm output, Macs have built-in DACs in their headphone jacks that typically support up to 96kHz — no external DAC needed for hi-res with wired headphones. Note: The offset effect requires headphones (stereo separation to each ear). Playing through speakers won’t produce the neurological benefit, though you can still enjoy your music.
The offset represents a timing difference in audio samples between your left and right ears. Higher numbers create a stronger effect. The defaults are 60 for lossy audio (MP3, AAC) and 120 for lossless (FLAC, ALAC). These values were chosen because lossless files typically have higher sample rates (96kHz vs 44.1kHz). Since the offset is measured in samples, not time, doubling the sample rate means you need roughly double the offset to achieve a similar perceptual effect. This keeps the experience consistent regardless of audio quality.

Beyond the immediate stress reduction and focus benefits, regular use may support neuroplasticity — your brain’s ability to form new neural pathways. IMA Type-A uses standard stereo headphones to deliver the offset effect through your ear canals. This is the mode available on iOS and works with any headphones, including haptic devices like bone conduction headphones. IMA Type-B adds a haptic device (tested with bone conduction) as a second output, delivering the offset through both air conduction (regular headphones) and haptic pathways simultaneously. This dual-pathway approach may enhance the neurological effect. Other haptic interfaces (wearables, tactile transducers) could theoretically work too. Type-B requires simultaneous audio output to two devices (e.g., USB headphones + Bluetooth bone conduction). Unfortunately, iOS artificially restricts this capability. Apple’s “multiRoute” audio category explicitly excludes Bluetooth, limiting dual output to their own Share Audio feature (AirPods/Beats only). Type-B will be available on macOS, which supports Aggregate Devices for true multi-output audio routing. If you’d like to see Type-B on iOS, consider filing feedback at feedbackassistant.apple.com requesting that Apple expand multiRoute to include Bluetooth A2DP.
